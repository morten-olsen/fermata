# Fermata — Vision

## The problem

Music listening has been in decline — not in hours played, but in quality of attention.

Streaming solved access. You can hear anything, anywhere, instantly. But in solving access, it destroyed something more valuable: the *experience* of listening. The ritual, the intention, the joy.

Modern music apps are built for engagement metrics, not for music. They interrupt you with algorithmic suggestions. They bury your library under editorial content and social features. They auto-play into infinite queues so you never have to choose — and never really listen. The interface is designed to keep you in the app, not to help you fall into the music.

The result: music became wallpaper. Something that fills silence rather than commands attention.

## The belief

We believe listening to music is one of the most joyful things a person can do — and that the tools people use to listen should honor that.

Every generation had a device that understood this:

- **Vinyl** made listening a ritual. You chose a record, committed to it, and sat with it.
- **The Walkman** made it personal. Your music, your headphones, the world disappearing.
- **The iPod** made your whole collection feel intimate. A focused path through your library, nothing else.

These weren't just playback devices. They shaped how it *felt* to listen. And that feeling — deliberate, personal, joyful — is what streaming lost.

## What Fermata is

**Fermata is a music player that restores the joy of listening.**

It is a calm, focused, beautiful app that puts your music front and center and gets everything else out of the way. No algorithms. No social features. No ads. No podcasts. No auto-playing infinite queues. Just your music, presented with care, waiting for you to press play.

The name comes from the musical notation 𝄐 — a held note. A deliberate pause. It means: *stop here, hold this, give it your attention*. That is what Fermata asks of the listener, and what it promises in return.

## Who it's for

Fermata is for people who still care about albums. Who have a library — scattered across Jellyfin servers, hard drives, maybe streaming services — and want it to feel like one collection again. Who want to sit down, pick an album, and *listen*.

It's for the person who remembers what it felt like to scroll the click wheel, or to flip through a record shelf, and wants that feeling back on a phone.

It is not for passive listeners. It is not for people who want the app to decide what plays next. It is not trying to compete with Spotify's discovery engine or Apple Music's editorial curation. It is the opposite of those things — by design.

## Core convictions

### Your music is yours

Fermata doesn't host music. It connects to where your music already lives — Jellyfin servers, local files, and eventually other sources. Your library, your data, your control. The app syncs metadata locally so browsing is instant and works offline.

### Albums are the unit of listening

An album is a complete thought. It has an arc, a sequence, a mood. Fermata treats albums as first-class objects: prominent artwork, full track listings, tap-to-play-front-to-back. The interface encourages committing to an album the way vinyl once did.

### Personal curation over algorithmic suggestion

Fermata will never tell you what to listen to. Instead, it gives you **mix tapes** — hand-built playlists that feel personal because they are. The act of picking tracks, ordering them, and pressing play is itself a joyful part of the listening experience. Mix tapes are the Walkman's cassette reimagined: your picks, your order, one tap to play.

### Calm is a feature

The app should feel like a quiet room with good speakers. Nothing competes for attention. Nothing interrupts. Nothing auto-plays. The interface waits for you — it doesn't chase you. This is a design choice at every level: no notification badges, no "you haven't listened in a while" prompts, no social pressure. Open the app when you want to listen. Close it when you're done.

### Play anywhere

Your music should follow you — not just to your headphones, but to your speakers, your home audio, your whole house. Fermata starts with local playback, but the architecture is built for output routing: send music to Music Assistant, Chromecast, AirPlay, or any system that can receive it. The phone becomes a remote control for your music, wherever it plays.

### Beauty serves function

Fermata is designed to be beautiful, but never decorative. Large album artwork isn't aesthetic indulgence — it's how you recognize and choose music. The dark palette isn't a style choice — it makes album art the brightest thing on screen. Every visual decision serves the listener.

## The long-term picture

### Phase 1 — Foundation (current)

A focused music player connected to Jellyfin. Album browsing, playback, search, mix tapes. One source type, one output (local device). The core experience is complete and joyful.

### Phase 2 — More sources

Local file playback. Plex support. The library grows. Deduplication across sources so the same album from two places appears once. The unified library becomes genuinely useful for people with music spread across systems.

### Phase 3 — More outputs

Music Assistant integration. The phone becomes a controller — tap play, and music comes out of your living room speakers instead of your phone. Chromecast, AirPlay, DLNA. Pick a room, pick an album, press play.

### Phase 4 — Deeper listening

Lyrics. Gapless and crossfade playback. Rich metadata from MusicBrainz. Artist bios and discographies. The app becomes the best way to *learn about* the music you're listening to — not through algorithmic recommendation, but through depth. Like reading liner notes.

### What Fermata will never be

- A streaming service. It connects to sources, it doesn't host music.
- A social platform. No profiles, no sharing, no followers, no "what your friends are listening to."
- An algorithmic recommendation engine. No "Daily Mix," no "Discover Weekly," no "Because you listened to X."
- A podcast app. Fermata is for music. Only music.
- An advertising platform. No ads, no sponsored playlists, no promoted content. Ever.

## The test

Every feature, every screen, every interaction should pass one question:

> *Does this help someone sit down and joyfully listen to music?*

If yes, build it. If no, don't. If maybe, wait until the answer is clear.

That's the vision. Everything else is execution.
